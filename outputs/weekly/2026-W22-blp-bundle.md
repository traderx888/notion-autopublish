(Bloomberg Economics) -- President Donald Trump faces a worsening energy dilemma. US consumers are

midterm elections are less than six months away. 
Trump has few tools to materially lower domestic energy prices, with one exception: restricting energy exports.
Administration oﬃcials have repeatedly dismissed the idea. Yet we think pressure is building, driven by political
optics and physical market constraints.
Americans are largely insulated from the global natural gas shortage. Prices remain low, and exports are
limited by midstream transportation and liquefaction capacity.
Similarly, a mismatch between US crude production and reﬁnery conﬁgurations means crude export curbs
would have limited impact on gas prices. While crude inventories are quickly falling and domestic
production has barely moved since February, we expect any curbs on crude exports to be limited — at
least until the US has exhausted its release of barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve in mid- to late-
summer.

least temporarily. Trump’s waiver of Jones Act requirements means more US consumers would beneﬁt.

allies and Trump’s energy-dominance narrative.

Trump Energy Export Curbs — Will He or Won’t He?: Fault Lines
By Megan O'Neil
(Bloomberg Economics) -- Welcome to Fault Lines, Bloomberg Economics’ daily research newsletter

With US midterm elections fast approaching, the Trump administration is under pressure to lower domestic fuel
costs. The White House insists it won’t restrict energy exports as the Iran war disrupts global supplies. Given
that presidents have been known to change their minds, today, we’re walking through some of the variables.  
President Donald Trump has already burned through his easy options on domestic energy prices. He waived
ethanol content rules, suspended Jones Act requirements and issued sanctions waivers for buyers of Russian
and Iranian crude. He is also working with Congress to suspend taxes on retail gas and diesel, a move that
would shave only a few cents oﬀ pump prices.
That leaves export restrictions as Trump’s main option for lowering prices before the midterms. The most
plausible target is reﬁned products, gas and diesel speciﬁcally. 
The US has a reﬁning mismatch problem — the crude it produces doesn’t match reﬁning capacity, meaning
export restrictions on crude itself wouldn’t help much. A broader export restriction on reﬁned products would
risk making some US reﬁners unproﬁtable. Limiting restrictions to gas and diesel would allow US reﬁners to still
sell other products like jet fuel and LPG to the global market while increasing supply of gas and diesel to the
US. And Trump’s suspension of the Jones Act would probably facilitate getting that supply to markets across the
US, reducing regional price variation.


The risks of any restriction are real, however. US product exports have increased by close to 2 million barrels
per day since the start of the Iran war. No other country comes close. Pull that supply from the global market
and the shock would ripple fast through Europe and beyond.
A narrower curb on gasoline and diesel exports would be less disruptive. Still, market distortions would likely
follow, with reﬁners incentivized to prioritize proﬁtable options like jet fuel. An even bigger risk? A US
restriction could kick oﬀ a round of export bans from other countries, further imperiling the ability of markets to
